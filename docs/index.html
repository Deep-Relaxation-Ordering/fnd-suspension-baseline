fnd-suspension-baseline

Brownian motion and gravitational sedimentation of diamond particles in aqueous suspension — a numerical pilot.

Current release: pilot-v0.3 Tests: 171 passed Stewardship: U. Warring, AG Schätz, Universität Freiburg

What this is

Fluorescent nanodiamonds (FNDs) in water are an emerging platform for cellular-scale sensing — their nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centres read out local temperature, magnetic field, and pH from inside biological samples. But before any of that physics is interesting, an experimentalist has to know whether the particles in their cuvette are actually suspended, partly stratified, or already on the bottom by the time the measurement starts.

This repository is the simulation that answers that question. Given a particle radius, sample temperature, cell depth, and observation time, it classifies a suspension into one of three regimes — homogeneous, stratified, or sedimented — and reports continuous boundary radii, fidelity envelopes, and design tables that an experimentalist can read directly.

The implementation is a numerical pilot: three independent solvers (analytical, Langevin ensemble, Smoluchowski PDE) cross-validate each other across a 30 × 7 × 5 × 6 parameter sweep. Every conclusion below is grounded in that sweep.

The long-horizon programme

This pilot is the first layer of a three-layer programme. Each layer closes one scientific question and earns the next major release; the programme’s navigation chart lives in docs/program-context.md.

L1 · Realism

Can a student plan a sealed-cuvette FND tracking experiment from this repository’s deliverables and cited references — with realistic aggregation, hydrodynamics, and polydispersity?

target: pilot-v1.0

L2 · Thermometry

Given an FND trajectory, does the simulator recover the local temperature consistently with NV-centre fluorescence on the same particle?

target: pilot-v2.0

L3 · Nucleation

Given an FND batch in a sealed micro-cell under a cooling protocol, can the simulator predict the distribution of nucleation temperatures and times within experimental error bars?

target: pilot-v3.0

Each layer is gated by a falsifiable acceptance criterion against an experimental campaign — not an internal "looks right" check. The scientific destinations sit on top of the simulator: it is the platform, not the prediction. See the layer detail and acceptance gates in the program context.

Key findings

The diamond–water system is gravity-dominated almost everywhere.

Across the breakout-note §5 sweep (6300 cells of r×T×h×tobs), about 45 % of cells are sedimented and only 18 % stay homogeneous. By unique experimental cell, 57 % of (r,T,h) tuples sediment at least once within a week; only 13 % remain homogeneous at every observation time. The headline experimental implication is that a tracking measurement on diamond is racing the clock against settling, not the other way around.

Cell depth is the dominant experimental knob.

The largest stay-mixed radius scales analytically as r ∝ h-1/3. A 100× change in sample depth shifts the homogeneous edge by exactly the analytic 4.64× ratio. Halving the cell depth is worth more than halving the particle size.

Temperature is a sub-bin secondary knob.

Across 5 °C → 35 °C, the homogeneous edge moves by ~3.4 %. The §5 r-axis (~10 % bin spacing) cannot resolve this: holding cell depth constant matters more than holding temperature constant.

Time matters more than temperature.

At fixed depth and temperature, the sedimented column more than doubles between 1 min and 1 h. Long-time distributions are set by the analytic equilibrium ratio; short-time distributions drift toward it. The classifier is bit-identical from ~4 h onward at most cells.

Two-thirds of the parameter space is closed-form.

In the §5 sweep, 67 % of cells are decided analytically — by the homogeneous-corner short-circuit, the equilibrated-corner short-circuit, or the asymptotic-sedimentation fallback. Only 33 % require the Smoluchowski finite-volume solver, and that one-third sets the wall time. Future tightenings of the radius axis are expensive; tightenings of the temperature or tobs axes are cheap.

Practical bracket for an experimentalist

At room temperature in a 1 mm cuvette:

Particle radiusBehaviour
r ≲ 11 nmStays mixed at any observation time up to a week.
r ≈ 30–250 nmVisibly stratified at hours.
r ≈ 50–250 nmVisibly stratified at minutes.
r ≳ 255 nmSedimented within 1 hour.
r ≳ 1.6 µmSedimented within 1 minute.

Changing cell depth shifts these brackets downward: the long-time homogeneous edge follows the analytic h-1/3 scaling; the finite-time sedimented edge moves more steeply with depth because both travel and relaxation times depend on it.

Implications for the wider exploration

The L1 layer matters even before L2 thermometry and L3 nucleation enter the picture, because every downstream measurement inherits the suspension state at the moment of observation. Three concrete consequences for the surrounding programme:

  • For NV thermometry experiments: a Brownian-motion measurement only makes sense in cells the simulator labels homogeneous or stratified at the chosen tobs. Sedimented cells are stationary by the time the camera captures the first frame; the apparent temperature read from track diffusivity is then a measurement of the wall, not the bulk fluid.
  • For nucleation studies: diamond surfaces as heterogeneous nucleation seeds need particles distributed through the cell at t = 0 of the cooling protocol — not concentrated against the bottom. The L3 question is bracketed by L1: the regime map tells you which (radius, depth, cooling time) combinations even have a meaningful nucleation experiment to run.
  • For the wider programme: the simulator’s job is to encode the physics literature already has, flag where measurement and theory disagree, and ground uncertainty bounds. It is not a replacement for measurements — a v3.0 result that disagrees with a careful experiment is a model failure, not a measurement failure. See programme-level falsification.
